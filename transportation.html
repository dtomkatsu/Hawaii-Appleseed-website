Transportation Equity

Safer streets, smarter transit, lower costs — so a car isn’t the price of admission to island life.

Research & News
Our Vision
“

Getting around shouldn’t cost a paycheck or a life.

Transportation is the second-largest cost for most Hawaiʻi families — behind only housing. And too many streets weren’t built to keep people walking, biking, or waiting for the bus safe. Working families pay the highest price: in fuel, in time, in lives.

A car shouldn’t be the price of admission to island life. Frequent transit and safe streets are how every family gets a fair shot.

We’re working to build a transportation system that doesn’t require a car payment to participate — frequent bus service, complete streets, and communities where work, school, and groceries are close enough to walk or bike to.

Frequent

Make transit truly frequent.

Bus service every 15 minutes — on every major corridor, all day. Predictable, reliable transit is the only kind people can build a life around.

Why this happens

The General Excise TaxGeneral Excise Tax (GET) — Hawaiʻi's tax on almost all business sales, set at 4.5% (4% state + 0.5% Honolulu county surcharge). Built into the price of nearly everything you buy. hits low-income families hardest.

A family earning $21,900 spends nearly all of it on essentials — food, rent, gas — every one taxed by the GET. A high-income household saves and invests a much larger share, which the GET never touches. Same tax, very different impact.

2026 Legislative Priorities

Frequent transit, safe streets, complete communities

Three areas where Hawaiʻi can lower the cost of getting around and make every commute safer.

1

Transit

Bus Network Redesign & Operating Support

Honolulu’s Bus Network Redesign proposes 15-minute service on every major corridor — but it needs sustained operating funding to deliver. State investment in city and county transit operations is the unlock.

2

Streets

Complete Streets & Safe Routes

Complete streetsComplete streets — roadways designed and built to safely serve all users: people walking, cycling, taking transit, and driving. Includes sidewalks, bike lanes, crossings, lower speeds, and accessibility features. design every roadway for pedestrians, cyclists, transit riders, and drivers — not just cars. Vision Zero, Safe Routes to School, and dedicated bike infrastructure save lives.

complete-streets projects already in the statewide pipeline

Land Use

Transit-Oriented & Complete Communities

Pair transit investment with housing and zoning reform so people can live close to where they work, learn, and play. Transit-oriented development around rail and high-frequency bus stops is the most cost-effective way to lower transportation costs at scale.

A Record of Results

Our Impact

A decade of transportation wins — from complete-streets policy adoption to coalition work on bus network redesign and Vision Zero.

2018

Helped pass statewide Complete Streets policy

A statewide commitment to design and build roads that safely serve everyone — pedestrians, cyclists, transit riders, and drivers — not just cars.

2020

Advanced Vision Zero in Honolulu

Coalition work to commit to eliminating traffic deaths and serious injuries — through design changes, lower speeds in residential areas, and protected infrastructure for vulnerable road users.

2022

Won Safe Routes to School expansion

Funding to bring sidewalks, crossings, and traffic-calming to school zones across the state — so keiki can walk and bike to school without their parents needing to drive them.

2023 – Present

Supporting Honolulu’s Bus Network Redesign

A once-in-a-generation overhaul to turn the bus from a coverage system into a frequency system — with 15-minute service on every major corridor. Coalition work to secure the state operating support that makes the redesign real on the streets.

2026

Aligning transit, housing, and land use

Working to pair sustained transit investment with zoning reform around rail and bus stations — the only combination that meaningfully lowers transportation costs at the household level. The data is unambiguous: transit-rich neighborhoods save households $3,000–$5,000 per year on transportation alone.
